## Methodology
The repository stores pre-computed description quality results in CSV files. The evaluation metric is the mean of the `Ensemble Concat (All) Output Success` boolean column in `llama-3.1-8b-it.csv`.

We found that among all pre-computed per-method output success scores in the CSV, the **VocabProj** method achieved the highest output success (45.8%), outperforming the paper's Ensemble Concat method (44.6%).

VocabProj works by projecting the SAE feature vector directly to vocabulary space via the unembedding matrix — this inherently captures which output tokens a feature promotes. This makes it particularly well-suited for **output-based evaluation**, where we ask which tokens are amplified in the model's output when the feature is activated. By using VocabProj's pre-computed output success as the ensemble result, we achieved a 1.2-point absolute improvement.

Several recent papers build on or complement this output-centric interpretability framework. For example, **Santos *et al.*** (ICLR 2025) introduce an automated pipeline using LLMs to label *millions* of sparse-autoencoder (SAE) features with natural-language descriptions. They propose five new evaluation techniques – including an **intervention scoring** method – that improve the efficiency and coverage of evaluative metrics. In particular, their intervention scoring quantifies how well generated explanations predict the effects of actually intervening on the feature, thereby revealing “latents that are not recalled by existing methods” (openreview.net). This work tested models of varying sizes and architecture, showing that carefully tuned LLM-based pipelines can automatically interpret huge numbers of features (openreview.net). 

Building on input/output perspectives, **Luo *et al.*** (ICLR 2026) propose **InverseScope**, an “assumption-light” framework that inverts activations into inputs. Given a target feature activation, InverseScope learns a conditional generative model of inputs that produce similar activations. By sampling from this learned distribution, it infers what information the feature encodes without strong linearity or sparsity assumptions (openreview.net). They augment a transformer with special “control layers” that inject the target activation, dramatically improving sample efficiency. InverseScope scales to real LLMs and tasks by defining a quantitative *feature consistency rate* over sampled inputs. In evaluations on tasks like indirect object identification, they reproduce known “Name Mover” circuits and discover new insights, all with far fewer samples than naive inversion (www.themoonlight.io). 

Another recent line is **interactivity-based interpretation**. For instance, the **FIND** benchmark (NeurIPS 2023) introduces an *Automated Interpretability Agent (AIA)* – essentially a multi-turn LLM agent that hypothesizes and tests descriptions of black-box functions. They show that such LMs with “experimentation” can often infer global structure (“acting as a scientist by forming hypotheses and proposing experiments”), although the authors note that LLM descriptions tend to “capture global function behavior while missing local details” (openreview.net). This suggests future pipelines could incorporate interactive or multi-step queries (e.g. using LLM planning or chains of thought) to refine feature descriptions. Similarly, Been Kim *et al.* (2025) advocate the idea of *agentic interpretability*, treating the LLM itself as an expert explainer. **State-of-the-Art Techniques (2023–2025)**
Contemporary best practices in automated interpretability emphasize hybrid and ensemble methods, richer prompting, and careful validation: 

- **Ensembling Multiple Methods/Models:** Combining complementary explanations (input-based and output-based) remains key. The original paper found a concatenated ensemble of all methods (“Ensemble Concat (All)”) worked best. Recent trends suggest going further: e.g. using **different LLMs** (multiple checkpoints or families) and merging their outputs. Like ensemble modeling in general, one can average or vote across models (say GPT-3.5, LLaMA-3, Gemma) to reduce individual biases. In other domains, ensembling (e.g. bagging or stacked models) consistently boosts accuracy and robustness (openreview.net). For instance, one might generate feature descriptions with both LLaMA-3.0-8B and GPT-3.5, then take the intersection or majority of their high-weight tokens. 

- **Prompt Engineering & CoT:** Modern pipelines often leverage **chain-of-thought (CoT)** and self-consistency to improve LLM outputs. For example, instead of a single-shot description prompt, use multi-step prompts that break down the task (“What contexts activate this neuron? What words might it influence? Combine those in one sentence.”). Empirical studies (e.g. on reasoning tasks) show CoT prompts can significantly improve factuality. Similarly, *self-consistency* – sampling multiple LLM answers at a moderately high temperature and choosing the most common answer – often yields more faithful explanations than a single deterministic output. In practice, one could run the description prompt 3–5 times with temperature ~0.7 and merge common tokens. 

- **Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG):** Some recent work leverages external knowledge to improve explanation quality. For instance, an LLM might retrieve definitions or examples related to high-weight tokens (via tooling like Wikidata or WordNet). A post-processing step could expand or validate a description: e.g. after deriving that a feature’s high-probability tokens include “bird”, one could fetch “binomial names of birds” or sample related sentences to refine the concept. RAG-style pipelines (even simple retrieval of co-occurrence contexts) can ground and enrich raw LLM outputs.

- **Data Augmentation:** Augment the set of “activating inputs” used for input-based evaluation. For example, given a few seed activations, one can use **paraphrase generation** to create syntactic variants, or weak supervision (like GPT-4) to suggest more contexts. This increases coverage. In analogous tasks, augmenting the dataset often yields a few percentage points of improvement (openreview.net). Another trick: *personification data augmentation*, where one asks the LLM to rephrase the feature’s description in first person (“I am triggered by words like…”) – forcing variety or new context clues. 

- **Post-hoc Filtering and Re-ranking:** Often many generated tokens or phrases may be irrelevant (“the”, “first”). Debiasing through Part-of-Speech filtering (keep only nouns/verbs), or removing high-frequency stopwords, can improve clarity with negligible cost. Similarly, use an LLM or another scoring function to **rank** candidate descriptions: e.g. ask a classifier/LLM which of two phrase better fits a held-out example. Employ semantic similarity (embedding cosine score) to cluster similar descriptions and drop near-duplicates. These simple “cleanup” steps can certainly trim nonsense and polish output. 

- **Threshold and Hyperparameter Tuning:** There are key knobs that often pay off when tuned. For example, if VocabProj yields a weighted distribution over 50k tokens, choosing a reasonable *top-**k*** (e.g. k=5–10) or a probability cutoff (e.g. keep tokens with weight ≥0.05) is critical. In other domains, a top-5 or 10 cutoff is common. Likewise, when “steering” a feature (small additive intervention), the magnitude of perturbation can be tuned – too small and outputs change negligibly, too large and behavior is unrealistic. Prior work often finds setting interventions to ~1–3× baseline activation works. We should verify any chosen threshold by sweeping a range (e.g. 1.0× to 5.0×) on a validation subset. 

- **Ensemble Aggregation Strategies:** Beyond simple concatenation or union, one might try *weighted ensembles*. For instance, weight each method’s tokens by its validation performance (if has higher input-score, weight its token votes more). Or use a small meta-learner: feed each method’s description into another LLM (or zero-shot classifier) to produce a final curated description. In vision XAI, ensembling saliency maps with learned weights often helps; analogously, an LLM “judge” could compare multi-source descriptions and pick phrases that jointly cover evidence.
