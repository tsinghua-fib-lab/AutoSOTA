## What Worked
1. **Antithetic variates ensemble** (K=25 pairs = 50 samples): By pairing each noise sample ε with its negative -ε, variance of the expectation is significantly reduced. This is well-established variance reduction technique from Monte Carlo methods. Produced the largest single gain (-4.1% vs baseline).
2. **Increasing ensemble size**: Going from K=3 (0.04246) → K=5 (0.04213) → K=10 (0.04196) → K=20 (0.04183) showed monotonically decreasing relative_l2 with diminishing returns.
3. **Sensor enforcement**: Overwriting output at sensor integer grid positions with exact sensor values gave a small additional improvement (~0.1%).

## What Didn't Work
1. **Changing ODE steps N**: The model is trained specifically for N=2 Euler integration. Increasing to N=5 (0.04818, much worse) or decreasing to N=1 (0.04544, worse) both fail. The flow matching objective trains the model to produce a specific velocity field calibrated for N=2 Euler steps.
2. **Heun's 2nd-order integration**: Catastrophically failed (relative_l2 = 0.322). The flow model is Euler-specific — different integration schemes break the model entirely.
3. **Trimmed mean of ensemble**: Slightly worse than mean (0.04175 vs 0.04174). The outliers removed by trimming are not actually harmful.
4. **K=50 antithetic pairs (100 total samples)**: Timed out (>1200 seconds). Compute bottleneck with large ensembles.

## Root Cause Analysis
The model is a flow-matching model that maps Gaussian noise → temperature field reconstruction via N=2 Euler integration steps. The primary source of randomness is the initial noise sampling, which introduces variance in the output. The main optimization lever is variance reduction in this ensemble, not ODE solver quality. The antithetic variates approach is optimal for this use case.
