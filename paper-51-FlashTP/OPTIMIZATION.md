**State-of-the-Art Techniques (2023–2025)**
Recent best practices in MLIP and related domains include: 
- **Mixed-Precision Inference:** Using FP16/BF16 for as many operations as possible. As shown above, casting dense layers to low precision can multiply throughput by several × (∼4× seen in MACE (chatpaper.com)). On NVIDIA Ampere/Hopper GPUs, enabling TF32 or BF16 tensor cores can give 2–10× speedups for matmuls (www.emergentmind.com). This requires careful accumulation and often keeping accumulators in FP32 to avoid accuracy loss (chatpaper.com) (www.emergentmind.com). 
- **Kernel Fusion and Compute Blocking:** Fusing multiple tensor-product steps into a single CUDA kernel reduces global-memory traffic. FlashTP itself fuses projection and tensor-product stages; beyond that, frameworks like TorchInductor or CUDA-Graphs can further fuse or streamline kernels. These techniques improve throughput by up to tens of percent (depending on how many passes are fused) and reduce launch overhead. 
- **Graph/Batch Parallelism:** *Batched simulation* and *multi-stream execution* can increase utilization. For GPU-bound workloads, running multiple MD images in one batch (bigger `target_edge_cnt`) or overlapping sequential operations can approach full device utilization. For example, batching 2–4 independent graphs often yields near-linear throughput gains if memory allows. Careful use of **cuGraph** capture can eliminate per-iteration launch overhead for static graph sizes. 
- **Compile-time Optimization:** Using PyTorch’s TorchScript or `torch.compile()` (TorchInductor) can optimize the execution graph. In principle, this may yield 10–30% improvement by JIT-compiling Python logic into faster kernels. However, custom C++ ops (like FlashTP’s kernels) and dynamic graph shapes may limit benefit or cause failure, so this technique has **moderate risk** of incompatibility. 
- **Profiling-Driven Tuning:** Running detailed profilers (nsight, torch.profiler) to identify memory bottlenecks and underutilized kernels. For example, one might find that half- precision arithmetic is idle on Ampere unless explicitly enabled, or that certain small tasks fall below the GPU’s efficiency threshold. Addressing these (e.g. by merging small operations) can yield incremental speed gains. 
- **MD-Specific Tricks:** In long MD simulations, **neighbor lists** usually persist between steps. Reusing or infrequently updating neighbor lists (rather than recomputing every step) can cut overhead. Similarly, exploiting crystal or molecular symmetries (rotating/mirroring coordinates) can sometimes reduce distinct computations. These can act like “augmentation” by reusing previously computed features, smoothing out noisy predictions. 
- **Ensembles and Uncertainty:** While typically used for accuracy (averaging multiple model outputs to reduce error), ensemble methods can inform early termination: if model outputs quickly converge across an ensemble, one can stop at fewer steps for MD. (This is a more speculative “post-processing” trick and may not directly boost raw speed.) 
- **Quantization (Post-Training):** Techniques from neural net inference – e.g. converting weights to 8-bit or 4-bit – can be applied post-hoc if supported by the hardware. There are early reports (beyond our domain) that 4-bit and 8-bit quantization can run nearly 2× faster on specialized hardware. However, MLIPs may be sensitive to quant noise, so this has **high risk** without calibration.
